# Letters to a Disciple
Abu Hamid Al-Ghazali · 12th Century

> knowledge without action is madness, and action without knowledge is vanity.

A short book that cuts deep. Al-Ghazali writes to a student who has asked for guidance, and the response is a series of direct, almost confrontational reminders about what really matters. Not how much you know, but how you live.

The book strips away the academic. It is not about theology as a system of propositions. It is about the soul. Al-Ghazali tells the student that a man may have read a thousand books and still be a fool if he has not acted on what he learned. The measure of knowledge is not memory, but transformation.

There is a line in there about how people spend their lives chasing status and comfort, and then death comes and none of it matters. It sounds bleak but it is not. It is liberating. If you know you are going to die, you stop pretending.

I think the best part of this book is how practical it is. It was written almost a thousand years ago but it speaks directly to the noise of modern life. It reminds you that the point is not to impress anyone. The point is to return to yourself.
