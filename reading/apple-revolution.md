# The Apple Revolution
Luke Dormehl · 2012

> Steve Jobs and his company represented a collision of technology and the liberal arts; they changed not just what we use, but who we are.

The Apple Revolution by Luke Dormehl is not your typical tech history book. It frames Apple's story through the lens of the counterculture movement  ;  the hippies, the hackers, the homebrew computer club. It explains how a company that started in a garage became the most valuable in the world not just through engineering, but through ideology.

There is a thread that runs from the Beat poets to the Whole Earth Catalog to the Macintosh. Dormehl connects these dots. He shows that Apple was never just about making computers; it was about making tools for creative people. The product is secondary to the philosophy.

The book covers the obvious beats  ;  Jobs and Wozniak, the Mac, the dark years, the return, the iPod, iPhone  ;  but the context around it is what makes it interesting. It talks about the cultural conditions that allowed Apple to happen. The California ideology. The belief that technology could liberate.

I think the best part of the book is how it treats Apple as a cultural phenomenon rather than just a business case study. It is easy to forget that before Apple, the computer was a machine for governments and corporations. Apple made it personal. That shift changed everything.
