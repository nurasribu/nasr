# The Essential Rumi
Jalal al-Din Rumi (trans. Coleman Barks) · 13th Century

> out beyond ideas of wrongdoing and rightdoing, there is a field. I'll meet you there.

This is a collection of Rumi's poetry translated by Coleman Barks. Rumi was a 13th-century Persian poet and Sufi mystic, but his work feels like it comes from outside time. It is about love, longing, the divine, and the absurdity of taking yourself too seriously.

The poems do not argue. They do not try to convince you of anything. They just open a door and invite you in. Some of them are short enough to fit on a napkin. Some of them go on for pages. They all circle the same truth: that the self is a veil between you and what is real.

There is a recurring image of the reed flute, separated from the reed bed, crying out. That is us. We are separated from where we came from and we spend our lives trying to get back. Every longing  ;  for love, for meaning, for God  ;  is really that homesickness.

I think the best part of Rumi is that he does not gatekeep. He is not writing for scholars or saints. He is writing for anyone who has ever felt lost. He does not give you a system. He gives you a doorway.
