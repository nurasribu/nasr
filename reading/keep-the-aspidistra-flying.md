# Keep the Aspidistra Flying
George Orwell · 1936

> He was a poet, and he hated the money-god.

A novel about a man named Gordon Comstock who declares war on money. He quits his job at an advertising agency to work in a bookshop for a pittance, moves into a miserable room, and tries to live as a poet unsullied by commerce. It goes badly.

Gordon is a hypocrite and he knows it. He despises the middle class but he was raised in it and he cannot escape its expectations. He wants to be free but he is too proud to be poor. The aspidistra of the title is a plant that sits in the window of every respectable English home. It represents everything Gordon is running from.

The book is funny and sad at the same time. Orwell writes Gordon with enough distance that you can see his self-destruction clearly, but also with enough sympathy that you feel it. There is a scene where Gordon spends his last shilling on a prostitute and then cannot look her in the eye. That is the whole book.

I think the best part of the book is that it does not give you an easy answer. Gordon eventually gives up and goes back to advertising, marries his girlfriend, buys an aspidistra of his own. Is that defeat? Or is it growing up? Orwell leaves it ambiguous.
