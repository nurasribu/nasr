# Create Dangerously
Albert Camus · 1960

> To create today is to create dangerously. Any publication is an act, and that act exposes one to the passions of an age that forgives nothing.

A lecture turned essay. Camus argues that the artist in the 20th century can no longer afford to be neutral. To create is to take a stand. The old model of the artist in an ivory tower is dead. The moment you publish, you enter the arena.

The book is short but dense. Camus talks about the relationship between art and politics, between the individual and history. He rejects both totalitarian art (art as propaganda) and art for art's sake (art as escape). The real work, he says, is to speak the truth without shouting, to bear witness without preaching.

There is a line about how the artist and the worker share the same condition: both are disposable under systems that value only what is useful. Art is not a luxury. It is a necessary rebellion against the absurd.

I think the best part of the essay is its quiet courage. Camus wrote this during the Algerian War, under threat from both sides. He knew that speaking honestly would make him enemies. He did it anyway. That is the example.
